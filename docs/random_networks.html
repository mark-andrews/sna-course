Erds-Rnyi network

  • Erds-Rnyi networks are networks produced by simple data generating process: Between each pair of vertices in an undirected graph of size \(N\), create a edge with fixed probability \(p\).
  • This is a \(G(N,p)\) Erds-Rnyi network.

Erds-Rnyi network

  • There are \[ {N \choose 2} = \frac{N(N-1)}{2} \] possible edges in an \(N\) vertex graph.
  • The probability that an edge is formed between any pair of vertices is \(p\).
  • The probability that an edge is formed between vertices \(i\) and \(j\) is independent of the probability that edge is formed between \(i^\prime\) and \(j^\prime\).

Erds-Rnyi network

  • We can represent the edges as a sequence of random variables \[ e_1, e_2 \ldots e_{N \choose 2}, \] each of which can take a value of either \(0\) or \(1\) independently and with probability \(p\).
  • Therefore, this is a Bernoulli process: \[ e_i \overset{\textrm{iid}}{\sim} \textrm{Bernoulli}(p),\quad\text{for $i \in 1\ldots {N \choose 2}$} \]

Erds-Rnyi network: Distribution of \(L\)

  • Number of edges has a binomial distribution: \(L \sim \textrm{Binomial}({N \choose 2}, p)\): \[ P(L) = {{N \choose 2} \choose L} p^L (1-p)^{{N \choose 2} - L}. \]
  • The average number of edges \(\langle L \rangle\) is \(p \times {N \choose 2}\).
  • The variance of number of edges: \(p \times (1-p) \times {N \choose 2}\).

Erds-Rnyi network: Density distribution

  • The graphs density is the number of edges \(L\) over the total number of edges: \[ \text{density} = \frac{L}{N \choose 2} = \frac{L}{\frac{N(N-1)}{2}} = \frac{2L}{N(N-1)} \]
  • Therefore, the probability of density \[ \frac{2L}{N(N-1)} \] is the probability of \(L\) edges.
  • The average density is \[ \langle L \rangle = \frac{p \times {N \choose 2}}{{N \choose 2}} = p. \]

Erds-Rnyi network: Degree distribution

  • For each of the \(N\) vertices, there are \(N-1\) possible neighbours.
  • There is a probability \(p\) that there is an edge each of the \(N-1\) neighbours.
  • These probabilities are independent.
  • Therefore, there is a binomial distribution over the degrees in the network: \[ P(k) = {N - 1 \choose 2} p^K (1-p)^{N - 1 - k}. \]

Erds-Rnyi network: Degree distribution

  • The average degree \(\langle k \rangle\) is \(p \times N -1\).
  • The variance of the degree is \(p \times (1 - p) \times N -1\).

Erds-Rnyi network: Poisson degree distribution

  • When \(\langle k \rangle \ll N\), then \[ P(k) = {N - 1 \choose 2} p^K (1-p)^{N - 1 - k} \approx \textrm{Poisson}(\lambda = \langle k \rangle), \] with \(\langle k \rangle = p (N - 1)\).

  • Vertex degree in binomially distributed: \(\textrm{Binomial}(N-1, p)\).

  • \(\langle k \rangle\) is \(p (N-1)\).

  • For low \(p\), degree distribution is approximately Poisson \(\textrm{Poisson}(\lambda = \langle k \rangle)\).

Degree distributions in real networks are not Poisson

  • In a Poisson distribution with mean \(\lambda\), the standard deviation is \(\sqrt{\lambda}\) and excess kurtosis is \(1/\lambda\).
  • If, for example, a friendship network was Erds-Rnyi, nearly everyone would have the same number of friends.

Erds-Rnyi network: Clustering coefficient

  • For a vertex \(i\) with degree \(k_i\), there are \[ {k_i \choose 2} = \frac{k_i (k_i - 1)}{2} \] possible edges in its neighbourhood.
  • The probability distribution over \(L_i\), the number of edges in the neighbourhood of \(i\), is a binomial distribution of size \({k_i \choose 2}\) and probability \(p\).
  • The average of this distribution is \(p \times {k_i \choose 2}\).

Erds-Rnyi network: Clustering coefficient

  • The clique coefficient of vertex \(i\) is \[ \frac{L_i}{{k_i \choose 2}} \]
  • The average value of this must be \[ \frac{p \times {k_i \choose 2}}{{k_i \choose 2}} = p. \]

Connectedness and supercriticality

  • If \(\langle k \rangle < 1\), or \(p < 1/N\), the largest component is approximately zero.
  • If \(\langle k \rangle \geq 1\), a giant component emerges.
  • If \(\langle k \rangle > \ln(N)\), the network is almost connected.

Watts-Strogatz small-world networks

  • In general, small world networks have low average distance: there are short distances between pair of nodes.
  • Many real world networks have this property, as do Erds-Rnyi graphs.
  • However, real world networks often do not have properties of Erds-Rnyi graphs.
  • Duncan Watts and Steven Stogatz (1998) proposed a variant of Erds-Rnyi graphs that they showed is small-world but has more properties of real wold networks.

Random graphs and degree distribution

  • Neither the Erds-Rnyi nor Watts-Strogatz networks have degree distribution typical of many real world networks.
  • Many real world networks friendships, sexual networks, www are heavy-tailed: some nodes have large number of connections.
  • More generally, the degree distribution is a power law: \[ P(k) \sim k^{-\gamma}. \]
  • Power law distributions are such that \[ \log(P(k)) \propto -\log(k) \]

Power law degree distributions

  • Networks formed by preferential attachment have power law degree distributions.

  • In preferential attachment, as a network grows, new nodes form attachments to nodes with many edges.

  • The probability of edge forming increases with the number of edges.

  • This is known as the Matthew effect,

    For unto every one that hath shall be given, and he shall have abundance (Matthew, 25:29)

    or the rich-get-richer.

Preferential attachment network

  • In a preferential attachment network, we start with one node and add new ones.
  • Each time we add a new node, the probability of an edge forming to a previous node \(i\) has the probability \[ p_i \propto k_i^\alpha, \] where \(k_i\) is the degree of \(i\).
